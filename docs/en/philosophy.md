# What is Yoga?

**Yoga is not just a sport, but a mindset and a philosophy.**

From my practice experience so far, I feel that: Compared to the muscular core emphasized in general fitness, yoga focuses more on the stability of the **internal organs**, the creation of space, and the placement of the Drishti (focal point). Compared to pure sitting meditation (which relies entirely on fine-tuning within the brain), the changes in yoga asanas are more visual and physical. In this regard, yoga is very similar to Tai Chi.

Therefore, the practice of Yoga Asanas (the part that looks most like a sport) is a way to enter into the philosophy of yoga. We must first seek form—deliberately striving outward for a standard posture or a higher-difficulty pose. Then we move on to exploring the essence (how it feels), figuring out the purpose of a particular asana: which part of the body is being stretched or stabilized. In which area is space being created? Finally, we shift our focus to our current physical and mental state, selecting the asanas that are most suitable for us at this moment to hold and deepen.

## The Five Basic Elements of Yoga Asana Practice

These five elements can be considered the foundation for our safe and deep practice: **Breathing, Extension, Stability, Space, and Drishti (Focal Point)**.
They are not merely theories on paper, but rather mental principles that need to be experienced step by step along our journey of practice.

### 1. Breathing

For beginners or those following yoga videos, we often hear cues like "Inhale" and "Exhale". Sometimes we might fall behind the rhythm or find our breathing direction is reversed. We need to understand that the ultimate goal of going to a yoga studio or watching yoga videos is to be able to practice yoga independently, peacefully, anytime and anywhere, based on the sensations of our own body. Therefore, we shouldn't be too stressed about the verbal cues (these directions often reflect the instructor's breathing rate or an average rate). Everyone's body is neither standardized nor average, and our body's condition is not constant either. So before trying to force ourselves to fit into a "standard", we should first observe and understand our true, present state: our breathing rhythm at this exact moment.

There is a general rule regarding breathing rhythm. Before entering an asana, find a comfortable seated position or come into a tabletop position (Chakravakasana) to establish your own breathing frequency and rhythm. **One inhalation and one exhalation counts as one breath; quietly count your own breaths.**

**Use the rhythm of your own breath as a measuring stick to determine how long you want to stay in different asanas**; **Whether you can maintain smooth breathing is the standard to judge if a certain asana is suitable for you right here and right now.** Only after assessing that your body and mind are ready, begin moving your body with small movements while maintaining awareness of your breath, warming up and activating different areas, and slowly increasing the range of motion to softly awaken the whole body.

> **Practical Experience**: There are many breathing exercises (Pranayama). For example, alternate nostril breathing (Nadi Shodhana) using the thumb and ring/little finger; or inhaling through the nose and exhaling deeply through the mouth as if blowing out a candle.
> When **inhaling**, you can try to bring the air deep into your internal organs (the abdomen); when **exhaling**, try to empty your entire abdomen. After emptying the abdomen, completely naturally, more air will flow in on the next inhale.

### 2. Extension & Stability

During asana practice, you can try to use your breath to connect your mind and body:
- **Inhale**: Feel an even extension upwards and forwards; feel the even lengthening of the front and back of the body.
- **Exhale**: Feel a sense of letting go and grounding downwards. Feel stability, safety, and support.

**Sheet-like Extension**: Yoga extensions (stretches) act across broad surfaces, not just isolated spots. Take the stretching of the back of the body as an example: it extends from the back of the knees, down the calves to the heels, and also from the back of the knees upwards to the back of the thighs (hamstrings), the glutes, the waist, and the entire back. It is a collective, unified elongation, not an isolated or intensely aggressive stretch of just one specific part. While focusing on the extension of one side, we shouldn't lose the space on the opposite side.

**Organ Stability**: Stability in yoga often refers to the stability found in supporting asanas or standing asanas. When supporting the body, the points of contact with the mat are usually the palms and the soles of the feet. Some supporting asanas also utilize the elbows or knees.
Stability here also relates heavily to the stability of the inner organs—feeling the self-support of the organs. Feel the levelness and stability of the hips; when standing, don't lean too far forward or backward. In supporting asanas, don't let the abdominal organs collapse downwards, but don't unnaturally arch them upwards either.

> **Practical Experience**: First, you can start by observing how your feet and hands make contact with the ground.
> - **Foot Support**: The triangle formed by the heel, the base of the little toe, and the base of the big toe roots firmly into the ground. When standing, feel the full activation of the inner, outer, and back areas of the thighs (especially the often-neglected inner thighs).
> - **Hand Contact**: The webbing between the thumb and index finger, along with the knuckles of all five fingers, make full, flat contact with the ground. The upper arms rotate slightly outward, which activates the inner upper arm, effortlessly transferring power from the shoulder blades through the palms down into the ground.

### 3. Space

While maintaining extension and stability, try to slowly create a little bit more "space". Explore and physically adjust just a little bit more during the process while keeping a smooth, flowing breath.
**This is a continuous process, not a final result.** It is a journey of steady refinement, not a destination reached overnight. Yoga is the process. We don't simply conclude asanas as whether we "can do it" or "cannot do it." The continuous effort itself is full of joy, hope, and ease; it's about gaining boundless perception within our finite bodies.

The continuous refinement in yoga is the pursuit of creating more space for the physical body, which slowly creates more breathing room for the mind. To borrow YogaLili's words: ***"We fail to do many asanas not because we lack strength, but because we lack space."***

> **Practical Experience**: A very clear physical realization of "space" occurs during the transition from Downward-Facing Dog (Adho Mukha Svanasana) when stepping one foot forward between the hands. This weight transfer requires the back of the standing body to already have sufficient extension. Then you slowly shift your entire center of gravity forward and softly place the foot down.
> What stops us from stepping our foot between our hands is actually insufficient flexibility (lack of space) in the back of our body, not a lack of core strength. If we place yoga blocks or a chair under our pushing hands, we can step forward much more easily. The height of the blocks or chair represents exactly the "space" and length that the back of our body "lacks" at this current moment.
> If this space is temporarily unavailable to you, you can stop where your foot lands and move your hands to either side of the front leg to adjust the back leg; or gently use your hand to physically move the resting front foot forward, slowly preparing to rise up to Warrior I or Warrior II.

### 4. Drishti (Focal Point)

Drishti acts as a wedge, an elegant guide for the posture. If we observe the "ultimate perfect appearance" of each specific asana, there will always be a specific Drishti instruction associated with it.

You can observe this principle in many common sports as well. For beginners, it might be exceedingly hard to feel exactly which muscle should be engaged or how to create a stable space. For example, when learning to snowboard (like the "pointing the way" technique): while sliding downward, beginners don't need to worry about micro-adjusting their center of gravity; simply following the gaze of their eyes smoothly initiates a basic weight transfer. The identical applies to skiing: where your eyes look is exactly where you will navigate.

So when we aren't yet very familiar with where our muscular connections should exert force, **the Drishti can subtly help us find a relatively comfortable and correct direction of exertion for our practice**. Concurrently, in yoga, the Drishti is also a conscious way to harness body energy, helping you to inward-focus and observe your body in the present moment.

**Common Applications of Drishti:**
- Upward-Facing Dog, Cow Pose: The Drishti is directed to the **sky (or upwards)**.
- Cat Pose: The Drishti scans the **navel**.
- Mountain Pose (Tadasana), Standing Forward Fold (Uttanasana): The Drishti softly rests on the **tip of the nose** (this gently draws the hyoid bone naturally back, allowing the deep digestive tract to ideally support the internal organs).
- Twisting Asanas: The Drishti is directed to the **back of the body**.

#### The Nine Drishtis in Traditional Ashtanga Yoga
1. **Midline and Facial Drishtis**
   * **Tip of the nose (Nasagrai / Nasagre)**: The most foundational focus, used in Tadasana, standing forward folds, etc., assisting in maintaining a calm heartbeat and naturally elongating the cervical spine.
   * **Third eye (Broomadhya / Ajna Chakra)**: The space between the eyebrows, often used in Fish Pose (Matsyasana) or during seated meditation, aimed at awakening deeper intuition.
   * **Navel (Nabi Chakra / Nabhe)**: Typically used in Downward-Facing Dog, helps to engage the abdominal musculature and leads the back chain into a fuller extension.
2. **Body Extremities Drishtis**
   * **Fingers / Palms (Hastagrai)**: The gaze follows the extension of the hand (e.g., in Warrior II), used to radiate body energy into the endless outward space.
   * **Thumbs (Angusthamadhyai)**: Commonly found when the arms are raised upwards in Sun Salutations, aiding in actively opening the chest and lungs to create upward space.
   * **Toes (Padhayoragrai)**: Used in seated forward bending asanas (Paschimottanasana), gently guiding the shoulders forward and preventing the loss of spine space due to dropping the head excessively.
3. **Directional and Spatial Drishtis**
   * **Looking Up / Toward the Sky (Urdhva / Antara)**: Used in Upward-Facing Dog, automatically guiding the front of the body to stretch evenly, creating deep-sensory awareness of a correct backward curve.
   * **Looking Left (Parsva)**: Used in spinal twisting asanas; the visual focus guides the neck naturally into a deep rotation.
   * **Looking Right (Parsva)**: Symmetrical with the left side, ensuring the body consciousness practice on both sides is balanced.

## 🧘‍♀️ Core Foundation: Donna Farhi's Organ Support Theory

*(This is the critical theoretical support running through all our specific weekly asana practices)*

During breathing regulation and when physically deepening in the positions, you can try to initiate the practice starting from the body's center, from the internal organ core. The underlying strength in yoga differs considerably from the strength in pure fitness training; it is nourished by the power of extension and the power of creating wide space. It is an organically originating **strength natively rooted in the internal organs**, rather than being driven in an isolated manner by superficial muscle mass.

### [When Supporting]
Inhale oceans of air deep into the abdomen, drawing it into deep communication with each internal organ, and feeling the extensive space inside the body. Upon exhalation, as the gas empties from the abdomen, actively feel your heart, lungs, stomach, and the small and large intestines being steadily wrapped and supported by the internal organ apparatus. Feel the solid carrying capacity of your very own digestive tract.

### [When Extending]
It is the absolute body center from the chest and abdominal cavities that takes the initiative to stretch and lengthen upwards, downwards, or side to side. Do not merely focus the awareness of stretching onto your outer limbs.

### [When Standing]
Sensitively feel the balance and stability of your organic level, much like a cup filled to the brim with water. Avoid leaning too far forward (letting the lower lumbar vertebrae sag into an excessive curve) and never slouch your back backwards like a valley.

### [When Twisting]
Rotations securely start from the organs at the body's core. In the active twist, we sequentially concentrate first on the stabilizing activation of our organ level, then organically expand that sensation widely across the spinal cord string, until the energy firmly covers the outer strands of your upper arms and calves.
