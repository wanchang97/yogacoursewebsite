# What is Yoga?

**Yoga is not just a sport, but a state of mind and a philosophy.**

From my current practice experience, compared to fitness which emphasizes the muscle core, yoga focuses more on the stability of **internal organs**, the creation of space, and the placement of the focal point (Drishti). Compared to pure sitting meditation (completely internal micro-adjustments in the brain), the physical movements in yoga asanas are more visual. From this perspective, yoga is very similar to Tai Chi.

Therefore, the practice of yoga asanas (the part that most resembles a sport) is a way to enter into the philosophy of yoga. First, we need to seek resemblance in form, deliberately seeking a standard posture externally or a more difficult posture externally. Then we ponder the essence, experiencing which part of the body this or this type of posture aims to stretch or stabilize. Which part of the space to build. Finally, paying attention to our current physical and mental state, choosing the posture that is most suitable for our current self to maintain and deepen.

## The Five Basic Elements of Yoga Asana Practice

These five elements can be said to be the foundation of our safe and deep practice: **Breathing, Extension, Stability, Space, and Drishti (Gaze)**.
They are not only theories on paper, but also inner mental frameworks that need to be gradually experienced step by step along the practice journey.

### 1. Breathing

For beginners or those following along with yoga, we often hear guiding words like "inhale, exhale". Sometimes we might not be able to keep up, or our breathing direction is opposite. You should know that our ultimate goal of going to a yoga studio for a class or watching a yoga video is to be able to practice yoga quietly by ourselves anytime, anywhere based on the feelings of our own body. Therefore, we don't need to be too nervous about the guiding words (the guiding words are usually more about the yoga teacher's breathing frequency or an average frequency). Nobody's body can be exactly "standard" or average, and our body state is not constant either. So before moving yourself towards a standard, first observe and understand your true state—your frequency at this moment.

There is a general rule for the rhythm of breathing. Before entering a posture each time, find a comfortable sitting posture or find your breathing frequency and rhythm in Tabletop pose (Chakravakasana). **One inhale and one exhale count as one; quietly count your breaths.**

**Use the rhythm of your own breath as a scale to measure the time you want to stay in different postures next; use whether you can maintain smooth breathing as a standard to judge whether a posture is suitable for yourself here and now.** After judging whether the body and mind are ready, then with awareness of breathing, rotate your body slightly to warm up and activate different parts, slowly increase the amplitude, and awaken the whole body.

> **Practical Experience**: There are many breathing exercises. For example, alternate nostril breathing using the thumb, ring finger, and little finger; exhaling through the mouth towards a candle.
> When inhaling, try to bring the air deeper into the internal organs (abdomen); when exhaling, try to empty the entire abdomen. After emptying the abdomen, more air will naturally flow in.

### 2. Extension & Stability

When practicing postures, try to use breathing to connect consciousness and the body:
- **Inhale**: Feel the even extension upwards and forwards, the even lengthening of the front and back sides of the body.
- **Exhale**: Feel the relaxation, rooting downwards, and feel stable, safe, and supported.

**Sheet-like Extension**: The extension in yoga is sheet-like. Taking the extension of the back of the body as an example, it is the overall lengthening from the back of the knee and the calf to the heel, and then from the back of the knee up to the back of the thigh, the back of the buttocks, the waist, and the back, rather than a single or particularly intense extension of a certain part. In the process of focusing on the extension of one side, do not lose the space on the opposite side.

**Organ Stability**: Stability in yoga is mostly stability in supported or standing postures. When supporting, it is generally the palms and soles of the feet that contact the mat. Some supported postures also use elbows or knees.
Stability is still related to the stability of the organs, feeling the self-support of the organs. Feel the level and stability of the hips; when standing, do neither lean forward nor backward. In supported postures, the organs neither collapse downwards nor arch upwards.

> **Practical Experience**: First, you can feel how your feet and hands contact the ground.
> - **Foot Support**: The triangle formed by the heel, the base of the little toe, and the base of the big toe roots firmly into the ground. When standing, experience the comprehensive activation of the inner, outer, and back thighs (especially the often-neglected inner thighs).
> - **Hand Contact**: The web between the thumb and index finger, and the bases of the five fingers contact the ground. Slightly externally rotate the upper arm to activate the inner upper arm, so that strength can be effortlessly transmitted from the shoulder blade to the palm and then to the ground.

### 3. Space

In the process of maintaining extension and stability, try to create some more space, and in the process of maintaining smooth breathing, explore and adjust a little more.
**This is a process, not an outcome**. This is a journey of continuous improvement, not an endpoint reached in one step. Yoga is a process, rather than simply drawing a conclusion of "can do" or "cannot do". This effort itself is full of joy, hope, and ease; it is the self-satisfaction of gaining infinite feelings within a finite self.

The continuous improvement of yoga is the pursuit of creating more space for the body, which slowly can also create a larger space for the mind. Borrowing words from YogaLili: *"Many postures we cannot do, not because we lack strength, but because we lack space."*

> **Practical Experience**: A very obvious feeling of space is the transition from Downward-Facing Dog stepping forward to between your hands. This movement requires the back of the body to already have enough extension, then slowly moving the overall center of gravity forward, and gently dropping the foot.
> What hinders us from stepping our foot between our hands is actually insufficient extension of the back of our body, not insufficient core strength. If we place yoga blocks or a chair under our hands, we can step forward more easily. The height of this block or chair is the space and length that the back of our body currently "lacks".
> If you don't have this space temporarily, you can stay where you can step to, move your hands to the sides of the front leg to adjust the back leg; or use your hands to pull the front leg forward to slowly prepare to enter Warrior I or Warrior II.

### 4. Drishti (Gaze)

The Drishti is a wedge, an ingenious guide. If we carefully observe the "ultimate perfect appearance" of each posture, there will always be a corresponding choice of Drishti.

This can also be found in many common sports. For beginners, they may not be able to well feel exactly which muscle needs to be mobilized, or how to create a stable space. For example, when learning snowboarding, there is a trick for beginners—in the process of going downwards, beginners do not need to care about the fine-tuning of the center of gravity; just following the movement of the eyes can complete the basic weight transfer. Skiing is the same; the direction of your eyes is the direction you are going.

So when we don't know much about the power generation positions of muscles, **the Drishti can ingeniously help us find a relatively comfortable and correct practice direction**. At the same time in yoga, Drishti is also a way to gather energy, helping you focus to observe the inner energy of your body at this moment.

**Common Applications of Drishti:**
- The Drishti for Upward-Facing Dog and Cow pose is the **sky**.
- The Drishti for Cat pose is the **navel**.
- The Drishti for Mountain pose and Standing Forward Bend is the **tip of the nose** (in order to push back the hyoid bone, so the digestive tract can support the internal organs).
- The Drishti for twisting postures is the **back of the body**.

#### Nine Drishti of Traditional Ashtanga Yoga
1. **Midline and Facial Drishti**
   * **Tip of the nose (Nasagrai / Nasagre)**: The most basic Drishti, used in postures like Mountain Pose and Standing Forward Fold, helps maintain inner peace and natural extension of the spine.
   * **Between the eyebrows (Broomadhya / Ajna Chakra)**: The "Third Eye", often used in Fish Pose or sitting meditation, aims to awaken intuition.
   * **Navel (Nabi Chakra / Nabhe)**: A typical application is in Downward-Facing Dog, helping to tighten the core and allowing the back to get a fuller stretch.
2. **Limb Extremity Drishti**
   * **Fingers / Palms (Hastagrai)**: The gaze follows the extension of the hand (such as Warrior II), used to guide energy to expand outward.
   * **Thumbs (Angusthamadhyai)**: Common in Sun Salutation's arms extending upward movements, helping to open the chest cavity and create upward space.
   * **Toes (Padhayoragrai)**: Used in Seated Forward Fold and other postures, guiding the body to stretch far, preventing the loss of back space due to over-lowering the head.
3. **Direction and Space Drishti**
   * **Looking up / Sky (Urdhva / Antara)**: Used in Upward-Facing Dog, guiding the front side of the body to stretch evenly, creating awareness of a slight backbend.
   * **Looking left (Parsva)**: Used in spinal twist postures, the line of sight drives the neck to complete a deep rotation.
   * **Looking right (Parsva)**: Symmetrical with the left side, ensuring balanced and aware practice on both sides of the body.

## 🧘‍♀️ Core Foundation: Donna Farhi's Organ Support Theory

*(This is an important theoretical support running through our specific posture practices each week)*

When breathing and performing each posture, you can start feeling from the center of the body, from the internal organs. The strength of yoga is different from the strength of fitness; the foundation of yoga is strength based on extension and space, a globally coordinated **strength originating from internal organs**, rather than being isolated and dominated by external muscles.

### [When Supported]
Inhale air deep into the abdomen, bring it to every internal organ, and feel the space inside the body. When exhaling, empty the air from the abdomen, feeling the heart, lungs, stomach, small intestine, and large intestine being stably supported. Feel the support of your own digestive tract.

### [When Extending]
Start actively extending and lengthening upwards, downwards, or to the left and right from the center of the body (chest and abdomen). Don't just focus on the extension of your limbs.

### [When Standing]
Feel the balance and stability of the body's organs, like a bowl of water. Do not lean forward excessively (collapsing the lower back and tilting the hips) or backward (hunching the back).

### [When Twisting]
The twist also starts from the organs at the center of the body. When supported, first become aware of the support starting from the organs, then expand to the back, and expand to the limbs.
