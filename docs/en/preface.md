I am Nayota Wuchang, an ordinary person who is both brave and open-minded, yet also sensitive and vulnerable. I often approach life with great seriousness and effort, but this overly effortful attitude frequently leaves me trapped in many things. I have many unrealistic desires and expect infinite possibilities of change from myself, but ultimately get lost in the process of this pursuit. For many years, I haven't been able to articulate what I truly want, nor do I know who I really am.

"Nayota" originates from Buddhist scriptures, meaning an immeasurable infinity. "Wuchang" (Impermanence) represents the cycle of formation, existence, destruction, and emptiness—the transient nature of things. This is a pair of relative concepts: the finite human life and infinite possibilities. In Zhuangzi's *The Secret of Caring for Life* (Yang Sheng Zhu), it is written: "My life has a limit, but knowledge is infinite." If we constantly strive to become a "better" version of ourselves, we can easily get lost in endless standards and external comparisons, not knowing which version is our "better" self. It seems as if every external "other" is a better version of ourselves, but we can never become someone else. Therefore, we need to observe our own feelings and simply try our best to understand our own uniqueness, our desires, and our fears. Perhaps then we will have the chance to stand up for ourselves and become a truer version of ourselves.

**My Connection with Yoga**
I started practicing yoga asanas during the pandemic. My initial method was to follow fitness app videos every day, training my core muscles and actively taking on the challenge of some high-difficulty yoga poses. Because I didn't warm up properly, I pulled my tailbone out of place. I only really started to rediscover and understand yoga about a month ago. When I signed up to become a yoga instructor for the 2026 semester, I started to think very seriously: What is it that I exactly want to share and let people feel when they come to my yoga class?

I began to delve into the details and sensations of foundational asanas and truly started to comprehend the philosophy and spirit of yoga. I hope to summarize my experiences and sensations from practicing yoga thus far, combining [Donna Farhi][donna]'s organ support theory and [YogaLili][lili]'s body-mind principles (breathing, grounding/rooting, centering), to help everyone practice yoga more safely and progressively step-by-step.
I will document my theoretical insights on this website, summarizing the entry methods for some foundational asanas. Then, in the offline practical sessions, I will explain these further to deepen the inner perception.
